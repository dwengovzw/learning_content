# Great principles

The *Great Principles framework* presents the core principles and core practices of computer science (Denning, 2009, 2003). 

> In other domains there are such “big principles” as well, such as the “big ideas” in the natural sciences ([Big Ideas](https://bigideasgreatscience.wordpress.com/)) corresponding to the “cross-cutting concepts” in STEM.

<div class="alert alert-box alert-success">
<strong><h5>7 principles:</h5></strong>
<ul>
    <li><strong>Computation</strong></br>Computer computations: capabilities and limitations of what can be computed with a computer</li>
    <li><strong>Communication</strong></br>Communication: sending data between digital systems</li>
    <li><strong>Coordination</strong></br>Coordination: collaboration among digital systems</li>
    <li><strong>Recollection</strong></br>Recollection: storing and retrieving data</li>
    <li><strong>Automation</strong></br>Automation</li>
    <li><strong>Evaluation</strong></br>Evaluation of effectiveness and efficiency</li>
    <li><strong>Design</strong></br>Design of systems</li>
</ul>
<br>
<strong><h5>5 practices:</h5></strong>
<ul>
    <li><strong>Programming</strong></br>Programming</li>
    <li><strong>Engineering of systems</strong></br>the process of developing, conceiving, and designing systems</li>
    <li><strong>Modelling</strong></br>Modeling</li>
    <li><strong>Applying</strong></br>Applying</li>
    <li><strong>Computational thinking</strong></br>Computational thinking</li>
</ul> 
</div>

It is noteworthy that programming stands apart from computational thinking, which aligns with the general view that computational thinking and programming are not the same.
